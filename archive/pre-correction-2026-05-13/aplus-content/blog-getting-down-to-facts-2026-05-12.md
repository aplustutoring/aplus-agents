### H1 + Body

# California Charter Intervention Tutoring: What the New "Getting Down to Facts" Report Means for Schools

*By A+ Tutoring Team · May 12, 2026 · 6 minute read*

California charter intervention tutoring is the unglamorous backbone of what works in school-level academic recovery. A new statewide research report has now made that conclusion impossible to ignore. The "Getting Down to Facts" research project, a 1,000-plus page assessment authored by more than 100 K-12 researchers and led by Linda Darling-Hammond (President of the California State Board of Education) and Susanna Loeb (Stanford University), calls on the state to require districts to hire more tutors, adopt evidence-based curricula, and scale interventions that already work in individual schools. For California charter directors and special programs coordinators who have been building or buying intervention services for years, the report functions as overdue validation. The harder question, and the one this article addresses, is what charter teams should actually do with that validation while the state catches up with the policy work.

## What the "Getting Down to Facts" report actually says

The Getting Down to Facts research initiative is published roughly every 10 to 12 years and serves as the canonical state-level diagnostic of California public education. The 2026 edition, [released in early May](https://calmatters.org/education/k-12-education/2026/05/california-schools-2/) and covered in CalMatters, spans more than 1,000 pages and synthesizes work from over 100 K-12 researchers.

The report's headline recommendation is structural: California's Local Control Funding Formula (LCFF) model lets effective interventions stay trapped in individual schools instead of scaling across the state. The researchers call on the state to require districts to adopt curricula with evidence behind them, hire more tutors and counselors, expand after-school programs, and stop letting every district reinvent the wheel.

For charter directors, that fourth recommendation is the operative one. California charter schools have been compensating for state-level fragmentation for more than a decade, either by building intervention programs in-house or by partnering with specialist providers. The schools getting the strongest results are usually the ones who picked partnership over rebuild-from-scratch. The Getting Down to Facts report now backs that choice with research.

## What does the report mean for California charter intervention teams?

A charter director or special programs coordinator reading the report has three immediate operational questions.

First, what changes about the authorizer relationship? In the short term, nothing. The Getting Down to Facts recommendations are advisory, not statutory. The California legislature would have to act on them. But charter authorizers (school district offices and county offices of education that oversee charter schools) are already aware of the report and are likely to reference it in 2026-27 charter renewal conversations.

Second, what does this mean for current intervention spending? Title I, Title III, and LCFF intervention dollars are already in the existing budget. The report does not move that money. What it changes is the political backdrop: the case for high-quality, evidence-based intervention is now state-research-validated, which strengthens the position of every charter team that has been advocating for specialist partnership over in-house pilot programs.

Third, what should be reviewed before next year's budget cycle? At minimum, current intervention contracts should be audited against ESSA evidence tiers and against the implementation criteria the Getting Down to Facts researchers identified. That audit is the practical takeaway from this report.

## What does the research base on tutoring actually show?

The Getting Down to Facts recommendation to hire more tutors is grounded in one of the most replicated findings in education research: high-impact tutoring (HIT) produces large and consistent learning gains when implemented with quality standards.

A [meta-analysis from EdResearch for Action](https://annenberg.brown.edu/sites/default/files/EdResearch_for_Recovery_Design_Principles_1.pdf), published through the Annenberg Institute at Brown University, identified the design principles that separate high-impact tutoring from supplemental homework help: small groups (1-on-1 to 1-on-4), school-day delivery, three or more sessions per week, trained tutors with sustained relationships, and ongoing progress monitoring. Programs missing any of these features show much weaker effects.

Stanford's [National Student Support Accelerator (NSSA)](https://nssa.stanford.edu/) reports that over 40% of schools nationally now offer HIT. NSSA's research summary documents that tutoring delivered three or more times a week by consistent, trained tutors using quality materials and data to inform instruction provides an average of more than four months of additional learning in elementary literacy and almost ten months in high school math.

These findings are not new in 2026. They are mature enough that bipartisan state legislatures are now codifying them. [FutureEd](https://www.future-ed.org/legislative-tracker-2026-state-tutoring-bills/) is currently tracking 41 high-impact tutoring bills across 19 states in the 2026 legislative session, including a Massachusetts proposal for $25 million in K-3 literacy tutoring grants and a Michigan bill (H.B. 5630) that would put $50 million behind a competitive grant program with research-aligned quality standards.

California has no comparable 2026 HIT funding bill. The Getting Down to Facts report is the closest thing the state has to a roadmap for that conversation.

## What does effective California charter intervention tutoring look like?

The intervention model the Getting Down to Facts researchers describe, and that the broader HIT research base validates, has a few non-negotiable design features:

- **Small group size.** One-on-one or 1-on-4 maximum. The evidence falls off sharply past 1-on-4.
- **During-school-day delivery.** After-school attendance is inconsistent, and students who most need help are often least available outside school hours.
- **Trained, consistent tutors.** Paraprofessionals and college-student tutors can deliver well-designed Tier 2 work, but only with training, ongoing coaching, and a curriculum framework.
- **Progress monitoring built in.** RIT-aligned MAP Growth assessments, ELPAC tracking for English Learners, or similar standardized growth measures.
- **Sustained relationships.** Same student, same tutor, over months. Tutor turnover is one of the largest predictors of weak HIT outcomes.

These are also what California's Getting Down to Facts report implicitly recommends scaling statewide. The state's existing intervention spending could already fund this model. The constraint is implementation, not money.

## What A+ Tutoring's partner schools are seeing

A+ Tutoring's California charter partner schools have been running this model since 2018. The outcomes match what the research predicts.

The clearest published example is A+'s iLEAD Math Tier 3 cohort case study. A+ worked with 12 students at iLEAD Antelope Valley (a publicly funded charter school in Los Angeles County, part of the iLEAD Schools network) over the 2024-25 school year. Each student received an average of 17 hours of one-on-one math instruction. By the end of the program, 9 of 12 students (75%) showed positive RIT growth on NWEA's MAP Growth assessment. The average gain across the cohort was 20.8 percentile points, with an average RIT increase of 18.7 points.

These numbers are not unusual for a properly implemented HIT program. They are unusual for what most California charter schools are getting from their current intervention model, which is precisely the scaling problem the Getting Down to Facts report names. The [full case study](/case-study-ilead-math-tier3) documents the program design and matches the HIT design principles outlined above.

## What charter directors and special programs coordinators can do next

The Getting Down to Facts report will work its way through California's policy cycle over the next 12 to 24 months. Charter teams reviewing 2026-27 intervention strategy can act sooner with three concrete steps:

1. **Audit current intervention delivery against the HIT design principles.** Who delivers Tier 2 and Tier 3 academic intervention? How often? With what training? How is progress monitored? Any unclear answers are the gaps to close.

2. **Map current intervention spending against ESSA evidence tiers.** Tier 1 evidence (randomized controlled trial validated) is what the Getting Down to Facts researchers are pointing to. Programs without ESSA evidence-tier documentation should be candidates for replacement or rebuild.

3. **Compare MAP Growth data by intervention tier, not just by grade or subgroup.** Schools running real Tier 2 and Tier 3 intervention should see disproportionate growth in their tiered cohorts. If the data does not show that, the framework is working but the delivery layer is not.
