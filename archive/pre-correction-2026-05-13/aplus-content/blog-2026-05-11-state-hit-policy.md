# How 19 States Are Codifying What California's Charters Already Run

*By Danielle Brodetsky, Director of School Partnerships, A+ Tutoring · May 11, 2026*

The 2026 legislative session has produced something rare in K-12 policy: 41 bills across 19 states, all converging on the same intervention model. FutureEd's legislative tracker shows state legislatures writing high-impact tutoring (HIT) into statute. Massachusetts is proposing $25 million for K-3 literacy. Michigan H.B. 5630 would put $50 million behind competitive provider grants with research-aligned quality standards. Connecticut H.B. 5032 carves out $5 million for high-dosage tutoring. The bills define what counts: trained tutors, consistent dosage, research-aligned materials, data-informed instruction.

California has no comparable HIT funding bill in the 2026 session. That isn't a knock on California's charter sector. It's the opposite. Charter directors and special programs coordinators across the state have been running this model for years, often without statutory recognition. The legislation arriving elsewhere is catching up to what the schools have already built.

## What's happening in the 2026 session

FutureEd's [2026 State Tutoring Bills tracker](https://www.future-ed.org/legislative-tracker-2026-state-tutoring-bills/) is following 41 bills across 19 states this session. The dollar amounts are not symbolic.

- **Massachusetts** is proposing $25 million for K-3 literacy high-dosage tutoring grants.
- **Michigan H.B. 5630** would appropriate $50 million for high-impact tutoring through a competitive grant program. Provider eligibility is tied to research-aligned quality standards.
- **Connecticut H.B. 5032** includes $5 million for high-dosage tutoring grants.

Three states. Over $80 million in proposed funding from one legislative cycle. That is before counting bills in the other 16 states the tracker is following.

The pattern matters because the bills are converging on a shared definition. "Tutoring" in statute now means something specific. It is not test prep. It is not after-school homework help. It is not unlimited-supply contracted tutors. It is intervention with quality standards attached.

Stanford's [National Student Support Accelerator (NSSA)](https://nssa.stanford.edu/), which has become the field's de facto standards body, reports that over 40% of schools nationally now offer HIT. The 2026 NSSA conference theme, "Human Connections in a Digital Age: The Next Chapter of High-Impact Tutoring," signals that the field has matured past the question of whether HIT works into the question of how to scale it well.

## Why this matters to charter directors and special programs coordinators

A charter director reading this is probably thinking one of three things. Either: "We already run something like this and no one noticed until now." Or: "We've been trying to run this but Tier 2 staffing keeps blowing up." Or: "Our intervention contract is up for renewal and I don't know how to evaluate the options."

The legislative wave changes the conversation for all three.

For schools already running specialist-delivered intervention, the bills offer a vocabulary for what was built quietly. When an authorizer asks for a 2026-27 intervention plan, Michigan H.B. 5630's quality standards become the spec that documents what the program already does. That makes for a fundable conversation with funders and a defensible conversation with authorizers.

For schools trying and struggling, the bills name what's missing. The recurring failure mode in school-built intervention isn't the framework. It is the delivery layer. Tier 2 academic intervention requires trained, consistent, data-informed staffing. When that layer is the part-time paraprofessional or the third period of a classroom teacher already managing 32 students, even an excellent MTSS framework does not produce HIT-level outcomes.

For schools evaluating intervention providers, the bills give a checklist. Reading Michigan H.B. 5630 alongside a current intervention contract is one of the most useful exercises a coordinator can do this month. The questions the bill is asking providers to answer are the questions to bring into the next vendor conversation.

## What the research actually says

The HIT evidence base is one of the most consistent in education research. A [meta-analysis from EdResearch for Action](https://annenberg.brown.edu/sites/default/files/EdResearch_for_Recovery_Design_Principles_1.pdf) identified the design principles that separate high-impact tutoring from supplemental tutoring: small groups of 1-on-1 to 1-on-4, school-day delivery, three or more sessions per week, trained tutors with sustained relationships, and ongoing progress monitoring.

NSSA's research summary puts the numbers in plain terms. Tutoring delivered three or more times a week by consistent, trained tutors using quality materials and data to inform instruction provides an average of more than four months of additional learning in elementary literacy and almost ten months in high school math.

The American Institute for Boys and Men's [research on high-dose tutoring](https://aibm.org/research/the-strong-positive-effects-of-high-dose-tutoring-for-boys-and-girls/) shows the model is one of the few interventions with replicable large effect sizes across student demographics. Harvard's Strategic Data Project recently released a [Measure, Monitor, Improve toolkit](https://www.gse.harvard.edu/ideas/usable-knowledge/26/02/high-dosage-tutoring-can-make-real-difference-harvards-strategic-data) for districts implementing HIT, in collaboration with the national nonprofit Accelerate and state departments of education in Delaware, Colorado, Ohio, Arkansas, and Louisiana.

The convergence in 2026 is not coincidence. State legislatures are not codifying a new idea. They are codifying a model whose evidence base accumulated over the past 15 years and is now mature enough that bipartisan legislative coalitions can defend it on paper. Michigan's $50 million bill, when it passes, will not fund pilots. It will fund provider networks that meet a quality bar the research community has already established.

## What's working

The model the bills are codifying has a few non-negotiable design features.

**Small group size.** One-on-one or 1-on-4 maximum. Evidence falls off sharply past 1-on-4. Whole-class "tutoring" is not high-impact tutoring.

**During-school-day delivery.** After-school tutoring shows much weaker effects, partly because attendance is inconsistent and partly because students who most need intervention are often least available outside school hours.

**Trained, consistent tutors.** Paraprofessionals can deliver well-designed Tier 2 work, but only with training, ongoing coaching, and a curriculum framework. Untrained volunteer tutors do not produce HIT-level outcomes.

**Progress monitoring built into the model.** RIT-aligned MAP Growth assessments, ELPAC tracking for English Learners, or similar standardized growth measures. Without a measurement layer, schools cannot tell whether intervention is working until annual state testing, which is too late to adjust.

**Sustained relationships.** Same student, same tutor, over months. Tutor turnover is one of the largest predictors of weak HIT outcomes.

These design features are what state bills are writing into statute. They are also what distinguishes intervention partners worth contracting from intervention partners worth replacing.

## What A+ sees in the field

A+ Tutoring's partner schools have been running this model since 2018. The outcomes are consistent with what the research predicts.

The iLEAD Antelope Valley Tier 3 combined Math and ELA program from the 2024-25 school year is the cleanest recent example. 21 students entered the program. 17 showed measurable MAP Growth, an 81% improvement rate. The average gain was 18.4 RIT points, roughly four to five times the expected rate for at-risk learners over the same intervention window.

The Math-only subset of the same cohort, which is the [published case study at wetutorathome.com](/case-study-ilead-math-tier3), shows the same pattern at a smaller sample. 12 students, 9 with positive RIT growth (75%), an average gain of 20.8 percentile points across the cohort, with an average of 17 hours of one-to-one instruction per student.

These are not outlier results. They reflect what the HIT design principles deliver when a school has the staffing, the consistency, and the data discipline to run the model correctly. The schools that get these outcomes don't get them by buying a curriculum or installing a platform. They get them by running the model the way the research describes it, and by having a partner who absorbs the staffing and delivery burden so the school can focus on student selection, scheduling, and academic ownership.

## What school leaders can do next

For coordinators planning 2026-27 intervention strategy, three concrete steps this month:

1. **Read Michigan H.B. 5630's quality standards.** Treat it as a vendor evaluation checklist. The questions the bill is asking providers to answer are the questions to bring into the next intervention provider conversation.

2. **Audit current Tier 2 academic intervention delivery.** Specifically: who delivers it, how often, with what training, and how is progress measured. If any of those answers is unclear, the Tier 2 layer is likely producing the kind of mixed results the [California MTSS evaluation recently surfaced statewide](https://blog.csba.org/ca-mtss/).

3. **Compare MAP Growth data by Tier, not just by grade.** Schools doing real Tier 2/3 intervention should see disproportionate growth in their tiered cohorts. If the data does not show that, the framework is working but the delivery layer isn't.

The policy wave will arrive. It usually does. The schools that lead are the ones that built the model before the law required it.
