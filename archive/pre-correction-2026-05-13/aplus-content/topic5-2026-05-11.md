## A+ Tutoring Company Post (LinkedIn)

**Audience:** Charter directors, special programs coordinators, district intervention leads
**Word count:** ~245

The state-funded evaluation of California's Multi-Tiered System of Support (CA MTSS) pilot is in. UCLA's Center for Transformation of Schools studied 35 schools across 26 districts over five years (2018-19 through 2022-23).

The behavioral results are real. Suspension rates dropped 1.2% overall in Phase 2B schools, with a 2.5% decrease for Black students in elementary. The framework is doing its job on school climate.

The academic results are not. Phase 2B Black students' math proficiency declined 16% over the same period, compared with a 5% overall decline for the same phase. ELA fell at most participating secondary schools. Math proficiency dropped 8-10% across phases. The Tier 2 academic outcomes the framework was supposed to produce did not materialize at the population level.

The takeaway isn't that MTSS doesn't work. It's that the framework is only half the work. Tier 2 academic intervention requires what the behavioral side already has: trained specialists delivering consistent small-group instruction with data-informed planning. Without that delivery layer, MTSS holds school climate together but doesn't close academic gaps.

For special programs coordinators planning 2026-27 intervention strategy, the pattern is familiar. Schools that built or partnered for specialist Tier 2 delivery are closing gaps. Schools that built MTSS as a structure but left Tier 2 staffing to existing teachers and paras run into exactly what the state evaluation describes.

The framework holds. The intervention delivery is the work.

Sources: UCLA Center for Transformation of Schools; CSBA Blog (CA MTSS Evaluation)

## Roman Op-Ed

**Pairs with:** Company post above
**Angle:** Conviction + system critique (the state built the easier half)
**Voice:** roman-voice v1.2
**Word count:** 135

California spent five years building MTSS frameworks across 35 pilot schools. The state evaluation just came out. Suspensions dropped, which is real and worth crediting. Academic proficiency went the other direction: math down 8 to 10 percent across phases, ELA falling at most participating secondary schools, Phase 2B Black students' math proficiency declining 16 percent versus 5 percent overall.

That isn't an MTSS failure. That's an intervention-quality failure with a behavioral framework on top of it.

You can't build Tier 2 with goodwill, paraprofessionals, and existing teachers stretched between 32 students. The behavioral side gets a discipline rubric and clear protocols. The academic side gets whatever the school can scrape together. The state evaluation just confirmed what the math always told you would happen.

The framework was the easier half of the work. The state built that half. Schools are still doing the other half on their own.

## Danielle Op-Ed

**Pairs with:** Company post above
**Angle:** Implementation / coordinator-credibility (the pattern from inside schools)
**Voice:** danielle-voice v1.2
**Word count:** 150

There's a pattern I see in struggling schools that the new California MTSS evaluation finally puts a number on. The behavioral framework is in place. The intervention specialists actually delivering Tier 2 academic instruction are not.

The state evaluation studied 35 schools across 26 districts and found suspensions dropped 1.2 percent overall, 2.5 percent for Black students in Phase 2B elementary. Those are real wins. Then it found Phase 2B Black students' math proficiency declined 16 percent over the same period. Two truths in one program.

MTSS works when the people doing Tier 2 academic intervention are trained for it and consistent over time. It does not work when Tier 2 is the part-time paraprofessional and the classroom teacher's third period. When coordinators tell me their MTSS data looks like the state evaluation, that is almost always what we find together.

The state evaluation just made the pattern visible at scale.
