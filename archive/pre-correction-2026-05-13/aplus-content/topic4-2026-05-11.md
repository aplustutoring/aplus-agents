## A+ Tutoring Company Post (LinkedIn)

**Audience:** Charter directors, special programs coordinators, district intervention leads
**Word count:** ~245

Stanford's SCALE Initiative just published its 2026 review of the evidence base for AI in K-12 education. The headline finding is one every charter director should sit with: **there are no high-quality causal studies of student AI use conducted in U.S. K-12 classrooms.**

That isn't a minor caveat. The review specifically searched for studies that estimate causal impact, the kind of evidence schools should require before spending intervention dollars on AI tools. What it found:

- Student performance often improves when AI tools are available during the task. Performance is mixed when students complete assessments without AI.
- AI tools with pedagogical guardrails (tutoring systems that give hints or guide reasoning) outperform general-purpose chatbots that just provide answers.
- Almost no research examines impacts on equity, student wellness, or social development.

While AI tutoring's U.S. K-12 evidence base remains thin, high-impact tutoring's keeps growing. NSSA's 2026 figures: over 40% of schools nationally now offer HIT, with research showing more than four months of additional learning in elementary literacy and almost 10 months in high school math.

The story matters this month. LAUSD just put every edtech contract under review for screen-time limits. The largest district in California is asking the question every district will be asking by next school year: what does the evidence actually say?

Sources: Stanford SCALE 2026 Review; NSSA / Stanford Accelerator for Learning; LAUSD via EdWeek Market Brief (May 2026)

## Roman Op-Ed

**Pairs with:** Company post above
**Angle:** Conviction + market critique (evidence base vs. vendor noise)
**Voice:** roman-voice v1.2
**Word count:** 125

Stanford just published the 2026 evidence review on AI in K-12. The headline: no high-quality causal studies of student AI use in U.S. K-12 classrooms. Zero studies that meet the bar.

That's the entire evidence base.

Meanwhile every AI tutoring vendor pitches "personalization at scale" to charter directors with intervention dollars to spend. The pitches saturate every conference. The Stanford finding is from this month. Both facts coexist because nobody in edtech is required to show their work before selling it.

High-impact tutoring isn't louder than AI tutoring. It's decades into showing actual outcomes. That difference is what schools spending real money should care about.

The research will catch up to AI tutoring eventually. The dollars spent in the meantime are the schools' bet, not the vendors'.

## Danielle Op-Ed

**Pairs with:** Company post above
**Angle:** Implementation / classroom credibility (what students actually learn from)
**Voice:** danielle-voice v1.2
**Word count:** 155

Every AI tutoring pitch I've sat through promises the same outcomes: personalization at scale, individualized learning paths, real-time progress monitoring, all without adding staff. The vendor decks are polished. The student outcome data is not.

Stanford SCALE just published its 2026 review of AI in K-12 education and confirmed something I've watched coordinators sense in those rooms. There are no high-quality causal studies of student AI use in U.S. K-12 classrooms. None. The evidence base I'd want before recommending any intervention to a school doesn't exist for this category yet.

The reason matches what I saw as a classroom teacher. Students don't learn from chatbots. They learn from a relationship with someone who knows them, sees their growth, and adjusts in real time.

When a coordinator tells me their school is considering an AI tutoring product, I ask one question: where is the U.S. K-12 outcome data? The question rarely has a good answer yet.
