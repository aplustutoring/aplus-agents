## A+ Tutoring Company Post (LinkedIn)

**Audience:** Charter directors, special programs coordinators, district intervention leads
**Word count:** ~245

The 2026 legislative session is reshaping high-impact tutoring policy nationally. FutureEd's legislative tracker counts **41 bills across 19 states** this session, with state legislatures codifying what the research base now confirms: trained tutors, consistent dosage, research-aligned materials, and data-informed instruction deliver gains that other interventions don't.

Three concrete examples for charter directors watching the policy environment:

- **Massachusetts** is proposing $25 million for K-3 literacy high-dosage tutoring grants.
- **Michigan** H.B. 5630 would appropriate $50 million for high-impact tutoring through a competitive grant program. Provider eligibility is tied to research-aligned quality standards.
- **Connecticut** H.B. 5032 includes $5 million for high-dosage tutoring grants.

Each bill is more than funding. It's a definition. When Michigan codifies "high-impact tutoring" into statute, legislators are writing into law the same design principles A+'s partner schools already operate on: trained tutors running consistent small-group sessions, RIT-aligned progress monitoring, data-informed planning.

California has no comparable 2026 HIT funding bill. The policy will arrive. Charter directors don't have to wait. NSSA's 2026 conference theme captures it directly: "Human Connections in a Digital Age: The Next Chapter of High-Impact Tutoring." Over 40% of schools nationally now offer HIT.

For intervention teams reviewing 2026-27 strategy, one useful exercise this month: read Michigan H.B. 5630 alongside your current intervention provider contract. The quality standards in the bill are doing the work of vetting for you.

Sources: FutureEd 2026 Legislative Tracker; Accelerate Model State Policy; NSSA 2026 Conference; Education Commission of the States

## Roman Op-Ed

**Pairs with:** Company post above
**Angle:** Conviction + system critique (legislature is choosing to lag)
**Voice:** roman-voice v1.2
**Word count:** 135

Forty-one bills. Nineteen states. The evidence base on high-impact tutoring is settled. The political will to write it into California law isn't.

Massachusetts is putting $25 million into K-3 literacy intervention. Michigan is putting $50 million behind quality standards that read like an honest spec for what intervention actually requires. California's 2026 session is busy with charter oversight bills that get vetoed and budget fights that consume the cycle.

There's no comparable California HIT bill this year. There won't be one next year either. The legislature isn't going to lead on this. It was never going to.

The charters running real intervention don't need a statute to tell them what works. They figured it out by doing it. The bill, when it eventually passes, will catch up to them.

California isn't behind on intervention. Its legislature is choosing to be.

## Danielle Op-Ed

**Pairs with:** Company post above
**Angle:** Implementation / teacher-credibility (coordinators recognize the design)
**Voice:** danielle-voice v1.2
**Word count:** 140

When you've sat with enough special programs coordinators, you can predict what they'll say when they read the Michigan high-impact tutoring bill. They'll recognize the design.

The design principles these bills codify are the same ones researchers, NSSA, and the Education Commission of the States have been writing for years: trained tutors, consistent dosage, research-aligned materials, data-informed instruction. That's the list every coordinator I work with has been trying to build inside their school, without a state law forcing the issue.

The schools that already have it didn't get there by waiting. They built or partnered until intervention met that standard. At iLEAD Antelope Valley last year, 21 students moved through our Tier 3 combined Math and ELA program. 17 of them showed measurable MAP Growth. Average gain of 18.4 RIT, roughly 4 to 5 times the expected rate for at-risk learners.

That's not a policy outcome. That's the work.
