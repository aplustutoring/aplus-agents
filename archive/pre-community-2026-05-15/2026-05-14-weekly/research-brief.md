### Topic 1 — Category A (Federal/state K-12 funding)

**Headline:** "The Title III clock is ticking: charter LEAs have until September 30, 2026 to obligate the EL and Immigrant dollars already in their accounts"

- **Why-now hook:** The 2024-25 Title III subgrant period ends September 30, 2026 — 4.5 months from today. After that, funds must be liquidated by December 30, 2026 or returned to CDE. The Title III Immigrant per-pupil rate for 2025-26 is $125.90. A+'s own target-schools list confirms specific unspent balances: Community Collaborative Charter sitting on $7,384 of Title III Immigrant funds, Cabrillo Point Academy on $3,813.
- **Roman angle:** Charter directors are debating whether to "do tutoring" while money already sits in their Title III accounts that will revert to the state in four months. The conviction frame: this is not a budgeting decision, it is a clock.
- **Danielle angle:** Operational playbook for routing Title III Immigrant funds into a Tier 3 small-group ELA intervention before the September 30 deadline. Name the spend categories CDE has already approved. Reference how partner schools have actually done it.
- **Target keyword:** "Title III Immigrant funds California charter schools"
- **Source URLs:**
  - https://www.cde.ca.gov/sp/ml/t3prelimalloctns.asp (CDE Title III preliminary allocations 2024-25)

### Topic 2 — Category B (K-12 problem stories)

**Headline:** "18,500 'fluent' California English learners get held back in EL status every year — and AB 2555 will not unstick them until 2027-28"

- **Why-now hook:** AB 2555 (English learners: reclassification) is currently moving through the California legislature. It would automatically reclassify students who score 4 on the ELPAC, but only commencing the 2027-28 school year. That leaves two full school years (2025-26 and 2026-27) where the bottleneck persists. Stanford-affiliated research found 39% of ELPAC-4 students in nine districts were not reclassified the next year. Statewide, about 18,500 ELPAC-4 students per year stay in EL status, ~18% of those who scored 4.
- **Roman angle:** Sacramento is acknowledging the reclassification machinery is broken, then giving the system a two-year head start before fixing it. Every quarter a student stays incorrectly labeled EL is a quarter of compounding mis-tracking.
- **Danielle angle:** The basic-skills requirement is where most students get stuck. A+ has a documented protocol for moving students past the basic-skills hurdle inside a single semester of Tier 3 intervention. This is the operational layer state policy keeps skipping.
- **Target keyword:** "long-term English learner reclassification California"
- **Source URLs:**
  - https://edsource.org/2026/california-english-learners-bill/753660 (EdSource, March 2026)
  - https://calmatters.digitaldemocracy.org/bills/ca_202520260ab2555 (AB 2555 bill tracker)
  - https://edpolicyinca.org/sites/default/files/2026-03/r_mercado-garcia_mar2026.pdf (PACE / Stanford research, March 2026)
- **iLEAD evidence fit:** Direct. The 87.5% ELA Tier 3 case study at iLEAD is exactly the intervention type that moves students past the basic-skills reclassification barrier.

### Topic 4 — Category D (Industry / competitor moves)

**Headline:** "Nerdy/Varsity Tutors still unprofitable in 2026 — the consumer-marketplace tutoring model is wrong for K-12 intervention"

- **Why-now hook:** Industry coverage as of early 2026 continues to flag Nerdy/Varsity Tutors as unprofitable, with B2C tutoring marketplaces hit by shrinking K-12 budgets and questions about whether the high-dose 1:1 economics ever worked. This is structural, not cyclical. Charter LEAs choosing intervention partners need a model not built on parent-funded 1:1 sessions.
- **Roman angle:** The 1:1 marketplace model never could pay for itself in K-12 because no parent can sustain that price point and no district can either. Small-group intervention funded by the school is the model that survives.
- **Danielle angle:** Why a Varsity-style B2C tutor cannot integrate with a charter school's IEP team, CAASPP cycle, or Title III routing. Operational case for the partner-funded model.
- **Target keyword:** "K-12 tutoring marketplace economics charter"
- **Source URLs:**
  - https://mediataylor.com/when-will-tutoring-businesses-turn-a-profit/ (industry analyst on Nerdy/Varsity profitability)

### Topic 5 — Category E (Research / academic)

**Headline:** "Stanford NSSA: high-impact tutoring drives a 3.1-day-per-year attendance gain — the chronic absenteeism crisis has a tutoring answer hiding inside it"

- **Why-now hook:** NSSA's D.C. attendance study found scheduled high-impact tutoring sessions decrease the probability of absence by 11.4% in middle school — about 3.1 more days of school per year if tutoring is part of the school day. PACE's "Unpacking California's Chronic Absence Crisis through 2024-25" reports California chronic absenteeism is still near 20%, roughly twice pre-pandemic levels. The two findings combined point at tutoring as a chronic-absenteeism lever, not just a score lever.
- **Roman angle:** Charter directors hire tutoring for academic gains. They also get attendance gains, IEP-team trust, and parent retention. That bundle is structurally underpriced in the market.
- **Danielle angle:** A+'s field experience matches the Stanford finding. Students who showed up for ELA Tier 3 sessions showed up to school more overall. Concrete protocol detail goes here.
- **Target keyword:** "high-impact tutoring chronic absenteeism"
- **Source URLs:**
  - https://nssa.stanford.edu/news/early-findings-show-evidence-high-impact-tutoring-increases-student-attendance-dc-schools (NSSA D.C. attendance study)
  - https://edpolicyinca.org/publications/unpacking-californias-chronic-absence-crisis-through-2024-25 (PACE absence report)
- **iLEAD evidence fit:** Available. The 12-student Math Tier 3 cohort and 8-student ELA Tier 3 cohort are exactly the dose and structure NSSA defines as high-impact tutoring.
