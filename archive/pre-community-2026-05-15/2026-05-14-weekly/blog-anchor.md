# Title III Funding Deadline 2026: What California Charter Schools Need to Do Before September 30

California charter schools have until September 30, 2026 to obligate their 2024-25 Title III English Learner and Immigrant funds. After that, unspent dollars must be liquidated by December 30, 2026 and any remainder reverts to the California Department of Education. The [Title III Immigrant preliminary per-pupil rate for FY 2024-25](https://www.cde.ca.gov/fg/fo/profile.asp?id=6249) is $125.90 per eligible student, per CDE's published funding profile. For a charter LEA with 50 newcomer students, that is $6,295 sitting in an account with a four-and-a-half-month clock on it. This guide explains what the Title III funding deadline requires, what counts as a valid obligation, and where the money is most productively spent.

## The dollars already in the account

Charter LEAs across California are sitting on Title III dollars they have not yet obligated. Multiple charter LEAs are showing four-to-five-figure unspent Title III balances in [CDE's most recent expenditure reports](https://www.cde.ca.gov/fg/aa/co/ca24winst3immytdexp6mo.asp). Any charter director can pull their LEA's report from the CDE Consolidated Application portal and see the current balance.

These are real dollars, already allocated and attached to verified Immigrant student counts. They do not roll forward. They are not discretionary funds the school can hold for next year. If they are not obligated by 11:59 PM on September 30, 2026, they revert to the state, and the EL students the funds were intended to serve lose a year of intervention the federal government already paid for.

The deadline is not a budget question. It is a clock.

## What does the September 30, 2026 Title III deadline actually require?

The Title III subgrant period for 2024-25 funds runs from July 1, 2024 to September 30, 2026. That window gives LEAs 27 months to obligate the funds. Obligate, in federal grant accounting language, means entering into a binding contract or commitment that creates a liability against the funds. Spending the cash is a separate step that follows obligation.

After the September 30 obligation deadline, LEAs have an additional 90 days to liquidate any encumbrances. That sets December 30, 2026 as the final cash-out date. Any funds neither obligated by September 30 nor liquidated by December 30 must be returned to CDE.

The same window applies to both halves of Title III: the English Learner program (formula-based for any LEA with identified EL students) and the Immigrant Student program (allocated separately based on newcomer counts). The [Title III Immigrant preliminary per-pupil rate for FY 2024-25 is $125.90](https://www.cde.ca.gov/fg/fo/profile.asp?id=6249) per CDE's published funding profile.

**What counts as a valid Title III obligation:**

- An executed vendor contract for supplemental services that begins before the deadline
- A purchase order issued for assessment or instructional materials beyond core curriculum
- A hiring action for a position fully funded by Title III, with start date before the deadline

**What does not count:**

- Funds set aside in a budget line but not yet committed to a vendor or hire
- A verbal agreement or handshake commitment
- A plan to spend in a future fiscal period

Schools should confirm specific obligation rules with their CDE contact or fiscal advisor.

## Why this matters for charter directors and special programs coordinators

Charter schools are statistically more likely than traditional districts to underspend their Title III allocation. The CDE expenditure reports for 2024-25 confirm the pattern at the six-month and twelve-month marks: districts run closer to their full allocation, charter LEAs trail.

The reasons are familiar to anyone running a charter business office. Smaller administrative teams mean no one whose full-time job is to route categorical funds; the school principal or executive director carries Title III as one task among many. Vendor identification is harder because Title III funds must pay for supplemental services, not the core instruction the school is already providing. That eliminates the curriculum the school already bought and the intervention the school's existing teachers already deliver. The remaining qualified vendor pool is small.

Audit risk avoidance pushes some business managers to spend zero, because spending zero never gets flagged in a monitoring visit. The unintended consequence is that the EL students Title III was designed to serve get nothing, and the federal allocation earmarked for them goes back to the state.

## What does the research say about how Title III dollars should be spent?

Stanford's National Student Support Accelerator (NSSA), led by Susanna Loeb at the Stanford Graduate School of Education, published findings from its work with D.C. Public Schools showing that scheduled [high-impact tutoring sessions decreased the probability of student absence by 11.4 percent for middle schoolers](https://nssa.stanford.edu/news/early-findings-show-evidence-high-impact-tutoring-increases-student-attendance-dc-schools). That works out to about 3.1 additional days of school per student per year when tutoring is part of the school day.

NSSA defines high-impact tutoring with specific dose conditions: at least three sessions per week, small group size of one to four students, sessions delivered during the school day, and the same tutor across the cycle. These are the conditions under which effect sizes hold up across replication studies.

The Learning Policy Institute, founded by Linda Darling-Hammond who currently serves as President of the California State Board of Education, has documented California's reclassification bottleneck: about 18,500 students per year score 4 (the proficiency threshold) on the ELPAC (English Language Proficiency Assessments for California) and remain classified as English Learners the following year. Title III funds are designed to pay for exactly the intervention layer that moves those students past the basic-skills hurdle the reclassification rules require.

## What works inside the supplemental-not-supplant constraint

The interventions that survive a Title III monitoring review share a profile. They are delivered outside the school's core instructional minutes. The provider is a vendor the school did not previously contract with for the same population. The instruction targets a Tier 3 caseload identified through CAASPP (California Assessment of Student Performance and Progress), ELPAC, or NWEA MAP Growth data, not the school's general population.

Synchronous online small-group tutoring fits this profile when structured correctly. The same design features that produce the learning gains NSSA documents also create the clean documentation paper trail Title III monitoring requires. Sessions logged with attendance records. Tutors logged as vendor employees, not school staff. Curriculum logged as supplemental, with a clear delineation from the school's adopted core program.

The charter LEAs that fully obligate their Title III allocation tend to be the ones with a standing relationship with a vendor who understands the supplemental-not-supplant rule, the CAASPP/ELPAC referral logic, and the 27-month obligation window.

## What charter directors and coordinators can do this week

Pull the school's latest Title III expenditure report from the California Department of Education's Consolidated Application portal. Confirm the unspent balance and the obligation deadline. The 6-month and 12-month CDE reports are public.

Identify which students would qualify for a Tier 3 intervention cohort funded by Title III. The English Learner side of Title III funds intervention for any identified EL caseload. The Immigrant Student side funds newcomer-specific services. Both can be combined for ELL newcomers in the same caseload.

Reach out to a qualified vendor before July 1, 2026. Contracting timelines in California charter LEAs typically run 4 to 8 weeks from initial scope conversation to executed agreement. A contract executed by the end of July still leaves time to obligate the full allocation before September 30.
