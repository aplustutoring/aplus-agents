# Dyslexia Screening Laws Hit Implementation Deadlines This Fall

Universal dyslexia screening is no longer optional in most of the country. California, Texas, Florida, Michigan, Connecticut, and more than a dozen other states have passed laws requiring K-2 dyslexia screening, and the implementation deadlines for fall 2026 are firm. Most charter LEAs have built the screening side of the rule. Few have built the intervention pathway that the screen is supposed to feed. Schools that screen 500 kindergartners in September and have no Tier 2 reading intervention in place by October are running the screen as paperwork. The kids stay where they were.

The fall 2026 deadlines create a procurement window that closes in early August for most charter LEAs. The screen runs the second week of school. Without a Tier 2 pathway in place before the screen results land, charter directors face the worst of two outcomes: full compliance with the screening law, and zero capacity to act on a positive result. The blog argues that any charter LEA implementing dyslexia screening for fall 2026 must lock the intervention pathway before August.

## What the Screening Laws Actually Require

Universal dyslexia screening laws differ in the specifics by state, but the core architecture is consistent. Schools must screen every student enrolled in kindergarten through second grade (in some states extending to third grade) using a validated screener for the phonological and decoding markers that predict dyslexia risk. The screen is administered within the first few weeks of the school year. Students who score below a defined threshold are flagged for further assessment and, critically, for tiered intervention.

California's SB 114 (signed 2023, implementation 2025-26) requires K-2 universal screening. The [International Dyslexia Association](https://dyslexiaida.org/state-dyslexia-laws/) maintains a current state-by-state tracker showing the screening windows, the approved screeners, and the implementation deadlines. Texas (HB 3928), Florida (SB 250), Michigan, and Connecticut have similar laws with 2025-26 or 2026-27 implementation. The [California Department of Education's dyslexia guidelines](https://www.cde.ca.gov/sp/se/sr/dyslexiaguidelines.asp) lay out the state-specific framework.

The laws are explicit about screening. Most are silent on what happens after a positive screen. That is where charter LEAs are quietly exposed.

## Why It Matters to Your School

A positive screen without an intervention pathway is a compliance liability and an educational malpractice problem at the same time. The compliance liability comes from federal law. Under IDEA, a school that identifies a student as at-risk for a specific learning disability and fails to provide appropriate intervention can face a child-find finding. The screen is a child-find action; ignoring its results is not.

> **Reflection question for the reader:** If your school screens 200 K-2 students this September and 30 score below the dyslexia-risk threshold, what is your written plan for those 30 students by Halloween?

The educational malpractice problem comes from the research. The window for catching and remediating early reading difficulty is narrow. According to the [National Center on Improving Literacy](https://improvingliteracy.org/brief/screening-essentials-students-risk-dyslexia), students who receive evidence-based reading intervention before the end of first grade have substantially better outcomes than students who wait until third grade or later. Schools that flag at-risk students in September and provide no intervention through the year are choosing the worse trajectory by inaction.

## What the Research Says About the Intervention Pathway

The research on what works for dyslexia-profile students is unusually convergent. Structured literacy, delivered consistently, in small-group or 1:1 format, with cumulative review built into the dosage, produces measurable gains. The approach is sometimes called Orton-Gillingham, sometimes Wilson Reading, sometimes structured-literacy generic. The shared core is the same: explicit, systematic, multisensory instruction in the phonological and decoding skills the screen flagged.

As Linda Darling-Hammond, President of the California State Board of Education, has framed the broader research, "Early identification only changes outcomes when it is paired with sustained, evidence-based intervention." Identification without intervention is a paperwork exercise.

Susanna Loeb's work at [Stanford's National Student Support Accelerator](https://nssa.stanford.edu/news/early-findings-show-evidence-high-impact-tutoring-increases-student-attendance-dc-schools) extends this finding into high-impact tutoring conditions specifically. Students receiving structured-literacy intervention under high-impact tutoring conditions (consistent tutor, MAP-aligned dosage of at least 90 hours over the year, school-day or scheduled delivery) show effect sizes of 0.20 to 0.40 standard deviations. For a first-grader screened at risk in September, those effect sizes translate into the difference between reading at grade level by spring and reading below grade level into third grade.

The [Annenberg Institute](https://annenberg.brown.edu/recovery/tutoring) reaches a similar conclusion in its high-impact tutoring meta-analysis, with one additional point: the effect concentrates on the lowest-performing readers when dosage and consistency are sustained. Schools that fund intermittent intervention or rotate providers see smaller effects than schools that sustain the same tutor and dosage across the year.

## What School Leaders Can Do Next

For charter directors and special programs coordinators reading this in May, five steps land the intervention pathway in time for the fall screen:

1. **Confirm your state's screening deadline and the approved screener by May 27.** The [IDA state law tracker](https://dyslexiaida.org/state-dyslexia-laws/) and your state department of education page are the authoritative sources. If your state requires K-2 screening for 2026-27, the deadline is binding.
2. **Audit your current Tier 2 reading infrastructure by June 3.** Who delivers structured literacy now, in what format, for how many minutes per week. Most charter LEAs will find their current Tier 2 reading work is partial and not aligned with the dyslexia-risk profile.
3. **Build or contract the structured-literacy pathway by July 1.** Same-tutor, MAP-aligned, 1:1 or small-group, at least 30-45 minutes 3-4 times per week. Inside-the-school staffing (reading interventionist) or contracted Tier 2/3 tutoring both work. The architecture matters more than the provider.
4. **Run a tabletop screen-to-intervention drill by August 8.** Pick a hypothetical positive screen and walk the named student through the pathway: who flags them, who assesses them further, who places them, who delivers, who tracks outcomes, who reports back to the parent. Every charter LEA running screening should have a written answer to all six questions before September.
5. **For administrators and federal program coordinators:** check Title I Part A, Title II Part A, Title III LEP, and state intervention funding lines for allowable uses on K-2 reading intervention tied to dyslexia screening. Most charter LEAs have eligible funding already allocated; the line item is rarely flagged as "dyslexia intervention" because the federal categories use broader language. Document the use and the outcome data so the spending stands up to compliance review and reinforces the case for sustaining the line next year.

The work fits inside the next 90 days. The cost of inaction shows up in October when the screen results land and there is no place to send the flagged students.
