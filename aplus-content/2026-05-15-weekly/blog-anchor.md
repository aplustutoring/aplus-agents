# Federal Education Grants Withheld: What California Charter Schools Need to Plan For in 2026-27

Federal K-12 education grants are being withheld at unprecedented levels in 2026. The White House Office of Management and Budget is holding back more than $2 billion that Congress approved in February for over 30 separate K-12 and higher-education programs, spanning English learner support, after-school programs, and teacher professional development. For California charter LEAs that built 2026-27 intervention budgets around the assumption that approved categorical grants would flow, the budget math has changed. This guide tells charter directors and special programs coordinators which federal dollars are still moving, which aren't, and how to restructure the intervention funding mix before next school year starts.

## The funds in question

The February 2026 Consolidated Appropriations Act preserved the two largest formula grants charter LEAs depend on: Title I, Part A at $18.4 billion and IDEA at $14.2 billion. Both flow on the regular cycle. The volatility is in the smaller categorical and competitive grants that charter LEAs typically use for intervention, English learner programming, and after-school supports.

[EdWeek's reporting](https://www.edweek.org/policy-politics/trump-holds-back-2-billion-for-education-grants-what-will-happen-next/2026/05) names the pattern: more than $2 billion of approved K-12 dollars are being held by the White House office that oversees federal spending. The FY 2026 budget request proposes eliminating 30-plus programs outright. February's appropriations rejected that proposal, but the executive branch is now withholding the same dollars administratively.

The result for charter business offices: Title I and IDEA are still safe to plan around. Smaller categorical streams are not.

## What does the federal grant withholding actually mean for charter LEAs?

Withholding is different from elimination. Congress appropriated the funds. The dollars exist on paper. The executive branch is delaying disbursement, and the legal proceedings around the withholding are unresolved as of May 2026.

For charter LEAs, the operational effect is the same whether the funds eventually flow or not: the school cannot count on them for 2026-27 budget commitments made now. A vendor contract executed in May or June that assumes a Title III award arriving in August carries risk that has not been there in prior years.

The categories most exposed in the current withholding action include:

- Title III English Learner and Immigrant Student Program subgrants
- Title II, Part A teacher and leader professional development
- Title IV, Part A Student Support and Academic Enrichment grants
- 21st Century Community Learning Centers (after-school)
- Various competitive intervention grants

Categories still flowing on schedule:

- Title I, Part A (formula, $18.4B preserved)
- IDEA Parts B and C (formula, $14.2B preserved)
- School meal reimbursements
- State-funded intervention layers (LCFF supplemental and concentration grants, state intervention block allocations)

## Why this matters for charter directors and special programs coordinators

Charter LEAs are statistically more exposed to federal funding volatility than traditional school districts. The reasons are structural. Charter business offices are smaller, with less staff bandwidth to absorb a cash-flow disruption. Charter LEAs more often depend on categorical grants as a material share of their intervention budget rather than as a top-up. And charter LEAs typically execute vendor contracts on tighter timelines, which means a delayed federal disbursement creates a real procurement problem within the same fiscal year.

The honest read for 2026-27 planning: a charter director who plans intervention around the assumption that all approved categorical federal grants will flow on schedule is taking a risk that did not exist before 2025. The directors who restructured their funding mix this spring around the formula grants and state-funded layers will not feel the federal volatility the same way.

That is not a partisan observation. It is a procurement reality. The federal volatility may resolve in court or in future appropriations cycles. Until it does, the safer planning assumption is that smaller categorical grants are a contingent revenue source.

## What does the research say about intervention that holds up across funding cycles?

Stanford's National Student Support Accelerator (NSSA), led by Susanna Loeb at the Stanford Graduate School of Education, has documented the conditions under which tutoring effect sizes hold up across replication studies: at least three sessions per week, small group size of one to four students, sessions delivered during the school day, and the same tutor across the cycle. NSSA also [found](https://nssa.stanford.edu/news/early-findings-show-evidence-high-impact-tutoring-increases-student-attendance-dc-schools) that scheduled high-impact tutoring sessions decreased the probability of student absence by 11.4 percent for middle schoolers, about 3.1 additional days of school per student per year.

NWEA's most recent post-pandemic recovery analysis tracks more than 5 million students. Per [K-12 Dive's coverage](https://www.k12dive.com/news/1-in-3-schools-recovered-in-math-or-reading-post-pandemic-nwea-reports/813284/), only one in three schools have recovered in math or reading; only one in seven have recovered in both. The "Rebounder" schools NWEA identified share a common profile: consistent intervention, school-day delivery, MAP-aligned dose, and a stable provider relationship. That profile is the operational definition of what the funding-mix decision is actually buying.

The Learning Policy Institute, founded by Linda Darling-Hammond who currently serves as President of the California State Board of Education, has long argued that intervention quality is a function of program design and staffing stability, not categorical funding source. The research is consistent: outcomes track operational design, not which federal grant code paid the bill.

## What works inside a volatile federal funding environment

The intervention models that survive a withholding cycle share three features. They are funded primarily through formula grants (Title I, IDEA) and state-funded layers (LCFF supplemental and concentration, state intervention blocks) rather than competitive or program-specific federal grants. They are sized to the formula-grant floor so that even if categorical layers are delayed, the program continues on schedule. They are documented under MTSS so that the supplemental-not-supplant requirement holds up regardless of which funding source contributed in a given quarter.

The charter LEAs that protect their intervention layer best in 2026-27 will be the ones that audit their funding mix this spring, identify which intervention dollars are non-volatile, and [size their vendor commitments to that floor](https://wetutorathome.com/heartwood-charter-school). Categorical grants that arrive can fund additional capacity. Categorical grants that are delayed do not collapse the program.

## What charter directors and coordinators can do this month

Pull the school's funding mix breakdown for 2026-27. Identify the floor: the portion that is formula-based (Title I, IDEA) plus state layers (LCFF supplemental and concentration, state intervention blocks). That is the non-volatile base.

Size the Tier 3 intervention commitment to the non-volatile base. Categorical federal grants that arrive in the fall fund additional capacity. Categorical grants that are delayed do not break the program.

Execute vendor contracts now, with payment terms that match the formula-grant disbursement calendar. Contracts that depend on a categorical-grant disbursement to start carry risk this year that they did not carry in 2023 or 2024.
