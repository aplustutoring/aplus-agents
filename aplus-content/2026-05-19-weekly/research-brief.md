## Candidate topics (one per category)

### Topic 1 . Category A (Operational charter calendar) . RECOMMENDED ANCHOR

**Headline:** Summer as a Tier 3 Runway, Not Summer School

- **Why-now hook:** Charter LEAs are making summer programming decisions in the next 21 days. Most are choosing between "summer school" (compliance-driven seat-time recovery) and "no programming." A third option exists: treating summer as a focused 6-8 week Tier 3 intervention runway for the 10-15% of students who fell behind during the year. Spring MAP windows closed this week, which means the cohort can be identified right now from data already in hand. Charter LEAs that commit to a Tier 3 summer runway in late May start the program July 8 with 8 weeks of dosage before the new year. Those that wait until July to decide miss the window entirely.
- **Roman angle:** Summer school as currently designed is babysitting with a teacher in front. Tier 3 summer runway is what should be happening: identify the kids who fell behind, give them a same-tutor, MAP-aligned, 6-8 week intervention block, and they walk into next September inside grade level. The fact that most charters don't do this is a planning failure, not a budget failure. The dollars are usually there in carryover. The will to use August on the right 30 kids isn't.
- **Danielle angle:** Implementation playbook for converting summer programming from compliance check-box to intervention engine. The cohort selection from spring MAP. The same-tutor consistency. The hours-per-student math (3-4 sessions/week x 6-8 weeks = 18-24 hours, which is at or above the iLEAD dosage threshold). The roundtable accountability: parent, director, tutor, A+ team aligned before week 1. Showing the verified iLEAD numbers as evidence the model works.
- **Target keyword:** charter school summer intervention program Tier 3
- **Source URLs:**
  - https://learningpolicyinstitute.org/product/effective-summer-learning-programs-report (Linda Darling-Hammond et al.)
  - https://nssa.stanford.edu/news/early-findings-show-evidence-high-impact-tutoring-increases-student-attendance-dc-schools (Susanna Loeb / Stanford NSSA)
  - https://www.brookings.edu/articles/the-effectiveness-of-summer-learning-programs-2024-update/
  - https://www.air.org/sites/default/files/Summer-Learning-Programs-Recovery-Acceleration.pdf
- **iLEAD evidence fit:** Direct. 75% Math Tier 3 / 87.5% ELA Tier 3 / 80% Combined Tier 3 outcomes were produced under the consistent-tutor, MAP-aligned, defined-dosage model that maps 1:1 to a Tier 3 summer runway. The 17-hour dosage threshold lines up with an 8-week summer block.
- **Distinct from May 15 + May 18:** May 15 was federal funding (Title III LTEL allocation). May 18 was state accountability (Dashboard / CSI). This week is operational programming (summer cohort design). No overlap.
- **National applicability:** Strong. Every state has charter LEAs making summer programming decisions in May/June. The framing is not California-specific.

### Topic 2 . Category B (Operational charter calendar)

**Headline:** Reading Spring MAP Data to Build Next Year's Tier 3 Cohort Before September

- **Why-now hook:** Spring MAP / NWEA assessment windows closed across the country this week. Most charter LEAs receive their reports between now and early June. The data is fresh enough to identify next year's Tier 3 cohort right now. Most charter directors put the report on their desk and re-open it in September. By then, the kids have been in summer mode for 10 weeks and the intervention starts at zero. Reading the data in May locks the cohort, lets summer programming target the right students, and lets September start with named students and an assigned tutor instead of with a meeting about which students need help.
- **Roman angle:** The data is sitting on charter directors' desks right now. The same kids who tested below grade level in spring will be tested below grade level in fall. The system pretends the summer gap is a data refresh. It isn't. Reading the data now and acting on it is what separates schools that move outcomes from schools that explain why outcomes didn't move.
- **Danielle angle:** Step-by-step protocol for reading a spring MAP report: identify students below 25th percentile in the subgroup that matters (Math K-2, ELA 3-8, etc.), cross-reference with IEP and reclassification status, build the Tier 3 cohort, name an intervention. The data work takes one afternoon. Most charter LEAs delay it five months.
- **Target keyword:** spring MAP data interpretation Tier 3 cohort
- **Source URLs:**
  - https://www.nwea.org/research/publication/the-state-of-the-american-student-2024/ (NWEA recovery research)
  - https://www.nwea.org/uploads/2024/01/Educator-Insights-Spring-2024-MAP-Growth-Norms-Report-NWEA.pdf
  - https://edsource.org/2026/spring-map-data-charter-schools/758912
- **iLEAD evidence fit:** Strong. iLEAD outcomes were achieved by reading the data, building the cohort, and assigning the dose. The same workflow applies.

### Topic 3 . Category C (Federal accountability / vendor selection)

**Headline:** ESSA's Four Evidence Tiers . How Charter LEAs Should Evaluate Tutoring Vendors

- **Why-now hook:** Spring procurement window for 2026-27 contracts opens in many charter LEAs in late May. Most charter directors do not know that ESSA categorizes intervention vendors into four evidence tiers (Strong, Moderate, Promising, Demonstrates Rationale) and that CSI/ATSI improvement plans must cite Tier 1-3 evidence. Picking a vendor without asking about evidence tier is a procurement failure. This blog walks through the four tiers, what each requires, and the exact question to ask any tutoring vendor.
- **Roman angle:** "Evidence-based" is a category most vendors throw around and most charter directors don't push on. Asking "which ESSA tier does your intervention meet, and where is the evidence" stops the bullshit at the first conversation.
- **Danielle angle:** A practical guide to the four tiers, with examples of what counts as Tier 1 (RCT-validated), Tier 2 (quasi-experimental), Tier 3 (correlational), and Tier 4 (rationale only). iLEAD outcomes are positioned as Tier 3 evidence approaching Tier 2 with the published case studies.
- **Target keyword:** ESSA tier 1 2 3 evidence tutoring vendor charter
- **Source URLs:**
  - https://www2.ed.gov/policy/elsec/leg/essa/guidanceuseseinvestment.pdf (USDE ESSA evidence guidance)
  - https://ies.ed.gov/ncee/edlabs/regions/southeast/pdf/ESSA-Tiered-Evidence.pdf (IES regional lab guidance)
  - https://annenberg.brown.edu/recovery/tutoring (Annenberg Institute high-impact tutoring evidence)
- **iLEAD evidence fit:** Direct. The verified iLEAD case studies (75% Math, 87.5% ELA, 80% Combined) are exactly the kind of correlational outcome data that ESSA categorizes as Tier 3. Positioning is honest and accurate.
- **Carryover:** Was the #1 follow-up seed from May 18's bundle. Strong evergreen candidate. Slightly less timely than Topic 1 because procurement windows are not all closing the same week.

### Topic 4 . Category D (Cross-state policy)

**Headline:** Dyslexia Screening Laws Hit Implementation Deadlines This Fall . What Charter LEAs Need to Build Before August

- **Why-now hook:** California (SB 114), Texas (HB 3928), Florida (SB 250), Michigan, Connecticut, and 12+ other states have universal dyslexia screening laws with 2025-26 or 2026-27 implementation deadlines. Most charter LEAs have not built the infrastructure to act on a positive screen. Screening without an intervention pathway is malpractice and exposes the LEA to compliance findings. The blog argues that any charter LEA implementing screening for fall 2026 must also have a Tier 2/3 intervention pathway in place before the screen runs.
- **Roman angle:** Screening exists to find struggling readers earlier. Schools that screen without an intervention pathway use the screen as paperwork. The kids stay where they were. The point of the screen is to act on it.
- **Danielle angle:** The pathway from screen-positive to Tier 2 reading intervention. What an Orton-Gillingham-aligned tutoring layer looks like. Why structured literacy fits naturally into a 1:1 tutoring runway. iLEAD ELA outcomes (87.5%) include students with dyslexia profiles.
- **Target keyword:** dyslexia screening charter school 2026 implementation
- **Source URLs:**
  - https://dyslexiaida.org/state-dyslexia-laws/ (International Dyslexia Association state law tracker)
  - https://www.cde.ca.gov/sp/se/sr/dyslexiaguidelines.asp (CA dyslexia guidelines)
  - https://improvingliteracy.org/brief/screening-essentials-students-risk-dyslexia (NCIL screening guidance)
- **iLEAD evidence fit:** Indirect. ELA Tier 3 cohort includes some dyslexia profiles but A+ does not specialize in dyslexia screening per se. Frame as adjacent rather than central.

### Topic 5 . Category E (Research)

**Headline:** Chronic Absenteeism Is the Tier 0 Problem . Why Intervention Doesn't Work If Students Aren't There

- **Why-now hook:** End-of-year attendance data is in. CA Dashboard makes chronic absenteeism a state indicator and 2024-25 nationwide rates remain around 26-30%. - **Danielle angle:** Practical pairing of attendance interventions with academic interventions. What the roundtable looks like when the school's attendance coordinator joins the intervention meeting. Data on iLEAD students with high vs low attendance to show the dosage-attendance interaction.
